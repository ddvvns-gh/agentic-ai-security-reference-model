Prevent AI outputs from becoming business commitments without the right authority, validation and evidence.

Output Commitment Control governs the transition from an AI-generated output to a real business action. It makes sure that an AI response, recommendation, generated file, API request, customer message, code change or transaction is treated according to its consequence.

The core idea is simple: AI may generate a proposal, but the enterprise decides when that proposal becomes an authorised commitment.

Purpose and strategic objective

Purpose

To control when AI-generated outputs are allowed to become authoritative, persistent, externally visible or operationally binding.

This capability ensures outputs remain in the correct state — draft, recommendation, pending approval, approved action, rejected action or committed transaction — based on business impact and risk.

Strategic objective

Enable safe autonomy by separating generation from commitment.

The enterprise can increase AI productivity while maintaining accountability, delegated authority, regulatory defensibility and operational control over material outcomes.

Architectural placement

Why this sits in Layer 2

Output Commitment Control is placed in Layer 2: Runtime, Data, Tool, SaaS, Memory & Cost Enforcement because the risk materialises while the agent is operating — at the moment an output is about to leave the model and become useful, visible, actionable or binding.

Identity controls determine who or what may act. Tool controls determine which tool may be used. Output Commitment Control determines whether the output is allowed to create business consequence.

This is the point where AI changes from “thinking or drafting” to “committing the enterprise.”

Why it matters

AI outputs are probabilistic

AI can produce plausible but incorrect outputs. A draft may be acceptable, but an automated commitment can create financial, legal, operational or customer impact.

Not all outputs are equal

A summary, customer response, code commit, payment instruction and regulatory filing require different levels of control.

Autonomy needs boundaries

Without this capability, agent autonomy can silently expand from assistance into unauthorised decision-making or action execution.

Problem statement

Traditional applications normally execute pre-defined logic. AI systems generate outputs based on inference, context and probabilistic reasoning. This makes it unsafe to treat every output as automatically correct or authorised.

Capability description

What the capability does

  • Classifies output type and intended use.
  • Determines commitment level and impact.
  • Evaluates output against policy and authority rules.
  • Routes outputs to allow, review, approve, reject or quarantine paths.
  • Records evidence for material decisions and commitments.
  • Prevents agents from self-authorising high-impact actions.

What the capability does not do

  • It does not guarantee that AI-generated content is correct.
  • It does not replace legal, compliance or business review where required.
  • It does not replace identity, tool, data or runtime controls.
  • It does not prove model reasoning; it records the decision and approval evidence.

Enterprise examples

Customer service agent

An AI assistant drafts responses to customer complaints.

Low commitment:
Generic troubleshooting guidance may be sent after basic policy checks.
High commitment:
Compensation offers, fee waivers or admission of fault require human approval.

Software engineering agent

An AI agent proposes and writes code changes.

Low commitment:
Documentation update or test generation may be auto-created as a draft pull request.
High commitment:
Production deployment or privileged infrastructure change requires change approval.

Procurement agent

An AI agent prepares vendor assessments and procurement recommendations.

Low commitment:
Draft supplier comparison is allowed.
High commitment:
Supplier award, contract approval or spend commitment requires delegated authority.

Regulatory reporting assistant

An AI agent drafts responses to regulatory information requests.

Low commitment:
Draft narrative or evidence pack may be prepared.
High commitment:
Submission to regulator requires accountable executive or compliance approval.
Failure scenarios

Unauthorised compensation

An AI assistant offers compensation above approved limits. The control should detect the commitment level and route to approval before the customer sees it.

Unsafe code commit

An AI engineering agent commits generated code directly into a protected branch. The control should classify the output as a production-impacting change and block direct commit.

Regulatory submission error

An AI-generated regulatory response is submitted without compliance review. The control should require formal approval and evidence before submission.

North-star vision

Every AI-generated output is treated as a proposed outcome until policy determines that it may become a draft, recommendation, message, change, transaction or binding decision.

The organisation can scale AI autonomy safely because material commitments are governed by risk, authority, evidence and accountability — not by the agent’s confidence alone.
