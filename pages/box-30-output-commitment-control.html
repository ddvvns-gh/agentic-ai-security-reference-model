Control the point where AI output becomes an enterprise commitment.

Output Commitment Control governs the creation, classification, approval, validation, execution and recording of commitments made or triggered by AI agents. A commitment is any action, communication, instruction, decision or transaction that creates an obligation, expectation, business outcome or externally observable consequence.

Purpose and strategic objectives

Purpose

Ensure that AI-generated or AI-triggered commitments are authorised, classified, registered, validated, traceable and attributable before they are released, executed or relied upon.

Strategic objective

Separate AI generation from enterprise commitment. The agent may propose, draft or recommend; the enterprise control plane determines whether the output can become a binding or externally meaningful action.

Architectural placement

Why this capability sits here

Output Commitment Control sits at the point where runtime AI behaviour turns into business consequence. It is downstream of prompt/output inspection and policy evaluation, and upstream of escalation, human accountability, decision lineage and assurance.

The model deliberately separates AI output from enterprise commitment. This is one of the key security and governance boundaries in agentic AI.
Trust domains

Decision Trust

Primary. Determines whether the outcome can be trusted, approved and relied upon.

Identity Trust

Validates who or what is acting, and under whose delegated authority.

Execution Trust

Ensures the commitment executes through controlled pathways and validated state.

Evidence

Captures registration, authority, threshold, approval, execution and lineage records.

Assurance

Tests whether commitment controls are designed and operating effectively.

Governance dependencies

Enterprise Commitment Threshold Matrix

Determines commitment category, impact level, authority requirement, approval level and escalation path.

  • Informational
  • Operational
  • Customer impacting
  • Financial
  • Regulatory
  • Strategic / executive

Delegated Authority Framework

Defines what commitments a human, agent, child-agent or workload may make, under what purpose, with what restrictions and for what duration.

Approval Matrix

Determines required approver level, dual-control requirements, segregation of duties and override authority.

Escalation Framework

Routes material, ambiguous, high-impact or policy-conflicting commitments to accountable human or executive authority.

Policy Framework

Defines prohibited, conditional and permitted commitment types across business, customer, financial, regulatory and operational contexts.

Decision Lineage Schema

Provides the evidence structure required to reconstruct why a commitment was proposed, approved, rejected or executed.

Reference architecture pattern

Agent Output / Tool Action → Commitment Detection → Commitment Registration → Classification → Threshold Evaluation → Authority Validation → Policy Evaluation → State-Locked Validation → Approval / Escalation → Execution / Release → Lineage & Evidence

Assurance model

Design effectiveness

Tests whether the control design would prevent, detect or evidence unsafe commitments if implemented correctly.

  • Are thresholds defined?
  • Are authority paths clear?
  • Is state-locking designed?
  • Is every commitment registerable?
  • Can lineage be reconstructed?

Operational effectiveness

Tests whether the control is actually operating in production and across all relevant commitment paths.

  • Are commitments registered?
  • Are approvals captured?
  • Are threshold decisions accurate?
  • Are exceptions monitored?
  • Are bypass attempts detected?

Continuous assurance

Automates control monitoring wherever practical and highlights exceptions for human review.

  • Registration coverage
  • Lineage completeness
  • Threshold compliance
  • State drift detection
  • Approval evidence currency
Enterprise scenarios

Customer fee refund

Agent drafts: “We will refund your fees.”

Desired state:
Commitment is registered, classified as financial/customer impacting, threshold is evaluated and approval is required.
Concern:
Agent sends the response as a casual message without registration, creating a hidden obligation.

Payment instruction

Agent initiates a payment or payment-like workflow.

Desired state:
High-impact threshold triggers authority validation, human approval and state-locked execution.
Concern:
Agent fragments payment requests to remain below approval thresholds.

Regulatory communication

Agent drafts or sends content to a regulator or external authority.

Desired state:
Regulatory threshold routes to accountable owner, evidence pack and formal approval workflow.
Concern:
Message is treated as informational but creates a formal enterprise position.
Maturity model

1 · Ad hoc

Commitments are not systematically identified or registered.

2 · Defined

Commitment categories and approval rules exist but are manually applied.

3 · Managed

Commitments are registered, classified and approved through defined workflows.

4 · Automated

Threshold, authority, state-locking and lineage controls are automated for material commitments.

5 · Assured

Continuous assurance validates registration, threshold compliance, lineage completeness and operational effectiveness.

Future evolution

Output Commitment Control should evolve toward dynamic thresholding, adaptive delegated authority, cross-agent commitment governance, AI-assisted assurance and automated detection of implied commitments in natural language. The north-star position is that every externally meaningful AI commitment is registered, threshold-evaluated, authority-validated, state-locked, approved where required, and reconstructable through evidence.
