Establish the system of record and lifecycle governance for every enterprise AI agent.

This capability treats AI agents as managed digital workers. It ensures every agent has a business purpose, accountable owner, lifecycle state, risk tier, operational status and retirement path before it becomes part of the enterprise AI workforce.

Purpose and strategic objective

Purpose

To maintain the authoritative inventory and lifecycle governance process for all AI agents, including proposed, approved, active, restricted, suspended and retired agents.

Strategic objective

Prevent unmanaged AI sprawl by making every agent visible, owned, risk-classified, lifecycle-managed and aligned to enterprise priorities.

Architectural placement

Why this sits here

This capability sits in Layer -4 because it is a business control plane capability. It determines whether an agent should exist before engineering, security or runtime controls are applied.

No agent should be engineered, deployed or operated without a business owner, risk tier and lifecycle state.
Problem statement

Enterprises will rapidly accumulate AI agents across business units, developer platforms, SaaS products and automation pipelines. Without a central lifecycle hub, agents become orphaned, duplicated, unowned or impossible to retire.

Capability description

What the capability does

  • Registers every AI agent with identity, owner, purpose and lifecycle state.
  • Maintains portfolio visibility across business units and technology platforms.
  • Supports approval, certification, restriction, suspension and retirement workflows.
  • Links agents to risks, controls, metrics, evidence and Prodspec records.
  • Enables portfolio rationalisation and reuse analysis.

What the capability does not do

  • It does not perform runtime security enforcement.
  • It does not replace identity or access management.
  • It does not determine detailed technical design for each agent.
  • It does not replace product ownership or risk acceptance.

Outcomes delivered

Known AI workforce

The enterprise knows which agents exist, where they operate and who owns them.

Lifecycle accountability

Agents are governed from proposal through retirement.

Portfolio discipline

Duplicate or low-value agents can be identified and rationalised.

Enterprise examples

Business-unit agent intake

A team proposes an agent to automate internal reporting.

Normal / desired state:
The agent is registered with purpose, owner, risk tier and approval pathway.
Risk / control concern:
The agent is built informally and later becomes a critical unowned process.

Retiring a legacy agent

An early proof-of-concept agent is no longer needed.

Normal / desired state:
The lifecycle hub triggers access revocation, data deletion and evidence retention.
Risk / control concern:
The agent remains active with stale credentials and unmanaged memory.
Failure scenarios

Unowned production agent

An agent continues operating after its product owner leaves the organisation.

Duplicate automation spend

Three business units independently build agents that solve the same workflow.

No retirement path

An agent is no longer useful but cannot be safely removed because dependencies are unknown.
