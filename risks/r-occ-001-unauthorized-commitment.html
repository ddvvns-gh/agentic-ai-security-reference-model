R-OCC-001

Unauthorized Commitment

An agent creates a binding enterprise commitment — a payment, contractual promise, external representation or other irreversible state change — that exceeds or falls outside its granted authority.

Risk IDR-OCC-001
CategoryDecision & Accountability
Trust domainDecision · Execution
ReversibilityOften irreversible
OwnerRisk (per box 33)
Parent capabilityBox 33 · Output Commitment Control
Description
A commitment is the point where an agent's output stops being information and becomes an obligation the enterprise must honour. This risk materialises when an agent issues such a commitment without — or beyond — valid delegated authority: promising a refund it cannot authorise, initiating a payment above its limit, agreeing contractual terms, or making a binding statement to a regulator or customer. Because the large-language-model component is not a security boundary, nothing inside the agent reliably constrains this; the exposure exists wherever a commitment can reach an execution path without an external authority check.
Threat scenario
A customer-service agent is steered — by indirect prompt injection in a customer message, or by ordinary reasoning error — into promising and initiating a goodwill payment well above any amount it is authorised to commit. The tool call to the payments API is well-formed and authenticated, so the connection succeeds. With no external, deterministic authority check at the commitment boundary, the payment executes and cannot be un-sent; the enterprise discovers the obligation only after the fact.

Residual risk
With C-OCC-001 enforced fail-closed at the commitment boundary, C-OCC-004 approval above thresholds, and high registration/classification coverage, residual risk reduces to Low–Medium. Residual exposure is dominated by classification miss (a commitment not recognised as one) and identity-lineage gaps; it rises to Medium where commitment classification (C-OCC-002) or agent identity lineage (Box 25) coverage is partial. The risk is never driven to zero because some actions are irreversible — hence the emphasis on prevention and human accountability rather than post-hoc rollback.
